DSLO Human‑Container — Cross‑Scale Coupling Geometry Map (v0.5)

---
Overview

The Cross‑Scale Map diagram visualizes how DSLO semantic geometry scales across multiple layers of human meaning. Unlike the other diagrams (which focus on individual‑level stability physics), this map shows how DSLO dynamics propagate, couple, and stabilize across:

*individuals

*dyads

*groups

*communities

*institutions

*digital systems

This is the multi‑scale semantic physics of DSLO — the geometry that explains why meaning behaves consistently across layers and why collapse or recovery in one layer can influence others.

---
1. Individual‑Scale Geometry
   
At the center of the diagram is the individual semantic manifold, containing:

*invariant core

*drift geometry

*load gradients

*collapse boundaries

*recovery pathways

This is the same geometry shown in the Container Model — but here it is explicitly treated as a single node in a larger semantic network.

---
2. Dyadic Coupling
   
Dyadic coupling shows how two individuals’ semantic manifolds interact.

It includes:

*shared boundary formation

*mutual orientation

*signal integrity coupling

*drift synchronization

*load transfer

Dyads are the smallest unit of cross‑scale semantic coupling.

---
3. Group‑Scale Geometry
   
Group‑scale geometry shows how multiple dyads form a shared semantic field.

It visualizes:

*group boundary formation

*collective coherence

*distributed fallback

*group‑level drift basins

*group‑level collapse modes

Groups exhibit their own drift, load, collapse, and recovery dynamics — distinct from individuals.

---
4. Community‑Scale Geometry

Community‑scale geometry shows how groups couple into larger semantic structures.

It includes:

*community boundary geometry

*shared meaning curvature

*distributed load envelopes

*community‑level collapse basins

*community recovery pathways

Communities amplify both stability and instability.

---
5. Institutional‑Scale Geometry

Institutions represent high‑curvature semantic structures with:

*rigid boundaries

*strong coherence constraints

*high fallback capacity

*large load reservoirs

*slow collapse dynamics

Institutional geometry can stabilize or destabilize lower layers depending on alignment.

---
6. Digital‑System Coupling

Digital systems form a parallel semantic layer that couples with human layers.

The diagram shows:

*digital boundary geometry

*signal integrity constraints

*cross‑layer drift propagation

*load amplification through digital channels

*collapse cascades across digital and human layers

Digital systems can accelerate drift and load across scales.

---
7. Cross‑Scale Stability Modes

The map visualizes stability modes that operate across layers:

*Local Stability Mode — individual stability

*Dyadic Stability Mode — relational stability

*Group Stability Mode — collective stability

*Community Stability Mode — distributed stability

*Institutional Stability Mode — structural stability

*Digital Stability Mode — computational stability

Each mode has its own invariants, drift geometry, load gradients, collapse boundaries, and recovery pathways.

---
8. Cross‑Scale Collapse Cascades

The diagram shows how collapse can propagate across layers:

*individual → dyad → group → community → institution → digital system

*digital system → group → individual

*community → dyad → individual

Collapse cascades follow geometric pathways, not random social dynamics.

---
9. Cross‑Scale Recovery Pathways

Recovery also propagates across layers:

*individual recovery stabilizes dyads

*dyadic recovery stabilizes groups

*group recovery stabilizes communities

*institutional recovery stabilizes entire semantic ecosystems

Recovery is upward and downward compatible across scales.
