DSLO Human‑Container — Drift Geometry Map (v0.5)

---
Overview

The Drift Map diagram visualizes how human meaning begins to deform when it moves away from invariant alignment. Drift is the earliest detectable form of semantic instability in DSLO. It is lawful, geometric, and predictable — and this diagram shows the exact structure of that deformation.

Drift is not collapse.
Drift is the pre‑collapse geometry.

This map shows drift basins, drift vectors, thinning feedback loops, divergence pathways, and the curvature conditions that lead toward collapse.

---
1. Drift Basins
   
Drift basins are regions of the semantic manifold where meaning begins to lose invariant alignment.

They represent:

*early‑stage instability

*curvature thinning

*weakening boundary conditions

*reduced coherence and continuity

A drift basin is the “first warning zone” in DSLO geometry.

---
2. Drift Vectors
   
Drift vectors show the directional movement of meaning as it deforms.

They illustrate:

*the path meaning takes under misalignment

*the gradient of instability

*the direction of semantic thinning

*the trajectory toward load accumulation

Drift vectors always point toward regions of higher curvature stress.

---
3. Thinning Feedback Loops
   
Thinning feedback loops are self‑reinforcing drift patterns.

They occur when:

*boundary weakens

*coherence fragments

*continuity breaks

*fallback is overloaded

These loops accelerate drift and reduce the system’s ability to self‑correct.

---
4. Divergence Pathways

Divergence pathways show how drift can split into multiple instability trajectories.

These pathways represent:

*branching instability

*multi‑vector drift

*divergence into collapse‑adjacent regions

*loss of directional meaning

Divergence is the geometric precursor to collapse.

---
5. Drift Curvature

The diagram visualizes curvature regions where drift becomes:

*harder to reverse

*more structurally damaging

*more load‑inducing

*closer to collapse boundaries

Curvature determines whether drift remains recoverable or becomes collapse‑adjacent.

---
6. Relationship to Load Geometry

Drift is directly coupled to load:

*drift increases load

*load increases drift

*drift + load accelerate collapse

The Drift Map shows the entry point into this coupling.
