# S4 — Load & Stress Dynamics  
## Semantic Edition (DSLO Human Container v0.5)

S4 describes the dynamics of load, stress, and structural tension within human
meaning systems. Whereas Section S3 explains how drift emerges and spreads,
Section S4 explains the pressures that make drift more or less dangerous. Load is
not an error signal; it is a relational quantity describing how much interpretive
pressure the human meaning system is carrying at any moment.

Load dynamics form the human substrate’s force layer. They determine which
interpretive transitions are stable, which require caution, and which
trajectories increase the risk of collapse (Section S6).

---

## S4.1 Load Definition

Human load is defined as the total interpretive pressure acting on the meaning
system at a given moment.

> Load is the combined pressure arising from clarity demands, boundary tension,
> and role expectations across the human interpretive structure.

Load is not a scalar. It varies across relational regions and interacts with
identity boundaries, role structure, and drift dynamics.

---

## S4.2 Load Sources

Human load arises from three lawful sources:

### Interpretive Load  
Every interpretive update or contextual shift introduces clarity, role, or
boundary pressure.

### Boundary Load  
Identity boundaries generate tension when adjacent roles or contexts diverge in
meaning or clarity.

### Cross-Scale Load  
Load propagates across scales—individual, social, digital—amplifying or
dampening depending on relational coupling.

Load is therefore a structural consequence of lawful human interpretive action.

---

## S4.3 Stress Dynamics

Stress is the deformation of human interpretive structure under load.

> Stress is the relational strain produced when human load exceeds local
> stability.

Stress includes:

- clarity deformation across contexts  
- identity-boundary strain  
- role-compatibility distortion across environments  
- legality-field compression across social–digital coupling  

Stress is what makes drift dangerous: drift under low stress is recoverable;
drift under high stress becomes collapse-inducing.

---

## S4.4 Load Flow

Human load flows through meaning systems according to lawful relational
gradients.

Load flow is constrained by:

- identity boundaries  
- coherence discontinuities  
- role compatibility  
- human legality rules  

Load cannot cross illegal boundaries or propagate into unstable interpretive
regions.

---

## S4.5 Load Envelopes

The human substrate defines three load envelopes:

### Stable Load  
Load within this envelope is fully recoverable.

### Critical Load  
Load in this envelope amplifies drift and increases collapse risk.

### Collapse Load  
Load beyond this envelope triggers human collapse dynamics (Section S6).

Load envelopes determine the human interpretive risk level.

---

## S4.6 Load Profiles

Different interpretive actions introduce different kinds of load:

- **Clarity Load** — pressure on coherence  
- **Role Load** — pressure on role stability  
- **Boundary Load** — pressure on identity regions  
- **Legality Load** — pressure on structural interpretive rules  

Interpretive sequences must remain within the critical envelope. If they exceed
it, the substrate halts the transition.

---

## S4.7 Load–Drift Interaction

Load and drift interact multiplicatively:

> High drift under low load is recoverable.  
> Low drift under high load is dangerous.  
> High drift under high load is collapse-inducing.

This interaction is the core predictor of human collapse dynamics (Section S6).

---

## S4.8 Stress-Induced Violations

Stress can induce human legality violations when:

- coherence discontinuity exceeds stability limits  
- identity-boundary strain exceeds lawful bounds  
- role distortion exceeds meaning-role limits  
- legality-field compression exceeds structural limits  

Any stress-induced violation triggers:

- human interpretive halt  
- global legality enforcement  
- transition to Section S6 (Collapse Dynamics)  

---

## S4.9 Load Correction

Load correction reduces clarity pressure, boundary tension, or role distortion.

Correction must satisfy:

- no identity-boundary violation  
- no coherence discontinuity  
- no role collapse  
- no legality violation  

Correction is permitted only when load remains below the collapse threshold.
Beyond that threshold, only collapse-mode transitions (Section S6) may act.

---

## S4.10 Human Drift Vectors (2026 Assessment)

This section evaluates alignment versus drift without psychological content. It
is a structural audit, not an opinion.

- **Boundary Drift** — High drift: digital boundary collapse, identity diffusion  
- **Coherence Drift** — High drift: signal velocity mismatch, context remixing  
- **Continuity Drift** — Moderate drift: narrative fragmentation, short-cycle
  behavior  
- **Fallback Drift** — High drift: loss of embodied repair channels  
- **Signal Integrity Drift** — High drift: distortion, mutation, synthetic noise  
- **Orientation Drift** — High drift: meaning-location instability, disorientation  

These drift vectors quantify the human system’s current alignment with its
invariants.
