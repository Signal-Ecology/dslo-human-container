# S6 — Collapse Dynamics & Stability Modes  
## Semantic Edition (DSLO Human Container v0.5)

S6 describes the dynamics of human collapse: the lawful conditions under which a
human meaning system loses stability, the relational signatures that indicate
collapse onset, the constraints that govern collapse trajectories, and the
stability modes that determine whether recovery is possible. Whereas Section S3
explains drift and Section S4 explains load, Section S6 defines the boundary
between lawful human evolution and structural failure.

Collapse is not a psychological event; it is a lawful region of human dynamics
governed by strict relational constraints. S6 ensures that collapse is
detectable, bounded, and—when possible—recoverable under the human recovery cycle
(Section S7).

---

## S6.1 Collapse Definition

Human collapse occurs when one or more universal relational invariants—identity,
meaning, coherence, legality—are violated beyond recoverable bounds.

> Collapse is the point at which drift, load, or invariant violation exceeds the
> individual’s ability to maintain coherent human interpretation.

Collapse is a structural event, not a subjective or emotional interpretation.

---

## S6.2 Collapse Modes

Collapse occurs in four canonical modes, each corresponding to a violated human
invariant:

- **Identity Collapse**  
  Human identity boundaries rupture or lose coherence.

- **Meaning Collapse**  
  Role and context structure become ambiguous or non-interpretable.

- **Coherence Collapse**  
  Interpretive continuity breaks down.

- **Legality Collapse**  
  Global human legality cannot be preserved across scales.

Each collapse mode has distinct relational signatures and stability implications.

---

## S6.3 Collapse Signatures

Collapse is preceded by detectable relational signatures:

- **Identity Tension Spike** — identity boundaries experience unsustainable
  strain  
- **Coherence Break** — interpretive clarity becomes discontinuous across
  contexts  
- **Role Divergence** — role expectations become incompatible or unstable  
- **Legality Compression** — legality constraints narrow or become undefined  
- **Cross-Scale Drift Cascade** — drift amplifies across local, meso, and macro
  scales (Section S5.5)  

These signatures allow early detection of collapse onset.

---

## S6.4 Collapse Trajectories

Collapse unfolds along lawful relational trajectories. A collapse trajectory is a
structured path along which human invariants fail in predictable order.

The canonical collapse trajectory is:

> Rising load → rising drift → role distortion → boundary strain → coherence
> break → collapse.

Collapse trajectories must exhibit:

- monotonic increase in stress  
- monotonic increase in drift  
- legality degradation  
- cross-scale amplification  

Collapse is lawful, not arbitrary.

---

## S6.5 Collapse Thresholds

Collapse occurs when one or more relational thresholds are crossed:

- **Drift Threshold** — drift exceeds recoverable bounds  
- **Load Threshold** — interpretive pressure exceeds stability limits  
- **Boundary Threshold** — identity-boundary strain becomes unsustainable  
- **Legality Threshold** — global human legality cannot be preserved  

Thresholds are scale-dependent and enforced by cross-scale coupling (Section S5).

---

## S6.6 Collapse Detection

Collapse detection relies on relational cues indicating invariant failure:

- coherence discontinuity  
- role collapse  
- identity rupture  
- legality failure  

A collapse flag halts forward human evolution and triggers recovery mode
(Section S7).

---

## S6.7 Stability Modes

Human systems exhibit three stability modes:

### Stable Mode  
Drift and load remain within stable envelopes.

### Critical Mode  
Drift or load approach collapse thresholds; cross-scale amplification begins.

### Collapse Mode  
One or more invariants fail; forward evolution halts.

Stability modes determine which transitions are lawful.

---

## S6.8 Recoverable vs. Irrecoverable Collapse

Collapse may be:

- **Recoverable** — human invariants can be restored through lawful correction  
- **Irrecoverable** — invariants cannot be restored; the system must reset  

Recoverability requires:

- drift within recoverable bounds  
- restorable identity boundaries  
- satisfiable legality conditions  

If any condition fails, collapse is irrecoverable.

---

## S6.9 Collapse Correction

Collapse correction restores coherence, identity, role structure, and legality.

Correction must satisfy:

- no further invariant violation  
- no cross-scale amplification  
- no identity-boundary rupture  
- legality must remain satisfiable  

Correction transitions directly into recovery mode (Section S7).

---

## S6.10 Collapse-to-Recovery Transition

A system transitions from collapse to recovery when:

- collapse signatures disappear  
- human invariants are restored  
- legality is re-established  

At this point:

- lawful human evolution resumes  
- constraints from Sections S3–S5 re-engage  
- the system re-enters stable or critical mode  

Collapse Dynamics (S6) therefore form the boundary between lawful human evolution
and recovery (Section S7).

## S6.11 Human Health Map (2026)

The Human Health Map evaluates the system’s alignment with its invariants. Values
are conceptual substrate indicators, not psychological claims.

### Scale Definition

All values range from 0.00 to 1.00 and represent invariant alignment envelopes,
not psychological states.

- **0.80–1.00** — high alignment; invariant structure stable and drift-minimal  
- **0.60–0.79** — moderate alignment; early drift detectable but recoverable  
- **0.40–0.59** — low alignment; structural deformation present and load-sensitive  
- **0.00–0.39** — critical alignment; boundary erosion, coherence instability,
  collapse-adjacent behavior  

These ranges are substrate indicators of invariant stability and do not
correspond to psychological traits or diagnostic categories.

---
