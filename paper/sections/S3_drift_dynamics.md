# S3 — Human Drift Dynamics  
## Semantic Edition (DSLO Human Container v0.5)

S3 describes the dynamics of human drift: how human meaning deviates from stable,
invariant-preserving interpretation; how such deviations spread through
relational and contextual structure; and how lawful human transitions detect,
bound, and correct them. Whereas S1 establishes human identity and meaning, and
S2 defines boundary and role constraints, S3 explains the lawful behavior of
deviation itself.

Drift is not a psychological failure mode; it is a structural feature of all
dynamic human meaning systems. S3 ensures that drift remains bounded,
recoverable, and coherence-preserving across Sections S1–S7.

---

## S3.1 Drift Definition

Human drift is defined as the deviation of identity or meaning from its stable,
invariant-preserving trajectory.

> Human drift is the relational displacement between the individual’s current
> interpretive state and its lawful, coherence-preserving invariant state.

Drift is not noise or error. It is a measurable relational shift relative to:

- human identity boundaries  
- role and context structure  
- clarity and coherence conditions  
- human legality rules  

All drift is evaluated within the human interpretive environment defined in
Sections S1–S2.

---

## S3.2 Drift Propagation

Human drift propagates through meaning systems according to lawful relational
rules. Propagation is shaped by:

- clarity gradients across contexts  
- identity-boundary tension between roles  
- role compatibility across interpretive domains  
- interpretive displacement across social and digital environments  

Propagation is constrained by:

- adjacency legality between signals  
- continuity of human coherence  
- stability of identity boundaries  
- global human legality conditions  

Drift cannot propagate across:

- incompatible identity regions  
- discontinuous coherence regions  
- legality-incompatible transitions  

Drift propagation is therefore a relationally bounded human process.

---

## S3.3 Drift Accumulation

Human drift accumulates when small, individually lawful interpretive deviations
compound into larger misalignments.

Accumulation occurs through:

- repeated clarity shifts across contexts  
- repeated role adjustments within environments  
- identity-boundary tension  
- legality stress across cross-scale coupling  

Accumulated drift must remain within recoverable bounds. If accumulation exceeds
the drift envelope defined in Section S2.5, the system enters a pre-collapse
regime (addressed in Section S6).

---

## S3.4 Drift Detection

Human drift detection is performed through relational cues indicating deviation
from stable interpretation. Detection includes:

- clarity loss in signaling  
- role ambiguity across contexts  
- identity-boundary tension  
- legality strain across interpretive sequences  

A drift flag does not imply collapse; it signals that contraction or correction
may be required.

---

## S3.5 Drift Correction

Correction is performed through lawful human transitions that restore alignment
without violating identity, meaning, or legality.

Correction must satisfy:

- no identity-boundary violation  
- no coherence discontinuity  
- no role collapse  
- no legality violation  

Correction is permitted only when drift remains within recoverable bounds.

---

## S3.6 Drift Halt Conditions

A halt is triggered when human drift exceeds the lawful envelope.

Halt conditions include:

- identity-boundary collapse  
- coherence discontinuity across contexts  
- role collapse across interpretive domains  
- violation of human legality  

Upon halt:

- the system enters human recovery mode (Section S7)  
- only lawful correction transitions may act  
- no forward interpretive transitions are permitted
