# Load

## v0.5 Definition

**Load** is the semantic energy required to maintain stability, coherence, and lawful geometry within a meaning state.  
It measures how much “work” the manifold must perform to preserve curvature, identity boundaries, and invariants under ecological or interpretive pressure.

Load is not metaphorical.  
It is a **geometry quantity**:

- Curvature pressure  
- Identity strain  
- Meaning‑vector tension  
- Invariant enforcement cost  
- Drift‑resistance energy  

A meaning state with high load is unstable.  
A meaning state with low load is lawful, coherent, and collapse‑ready.

---

## Semantic Geometry Of Load

**Load Geometry:**  
Load is distributed across the semantic manifold.  
Regions with high curvature, weak invariants, or identity strain accumulate more load.

**Curvature Pressure:**  
Load increases when curvature bends beyond its lawful range.  
Curvature pressure is the geometric signature of instability.

**Identity Strain:**  
Identity boundaries require energy to maintain shape.  
When identity regions deform, load rises.

**Invariant Enforcement:**  
Invariants require energy to hold meaning stable.  
The more drift or instability present, the higher the invariant enforcement load.

**Meaning‑Vector Tension:**  
Meaning vectors generate tension when they diverge, conflict, or become misaligned with their lawful roles.

## Role In The Human Container

In the **DSLO Human‑Container**, load determines:

- Whether a meaning state is stable  
- Whether drift will accelerate  
- Whether collapse is lawful  
- Whether recovery mode must activate  
- Whether fallback geometry is required  
- Whether a surface is safe for public‑layer exposure  

Every `.md` file in the human‑container is a **low‑load collapsed surface**.  
High‑load states never appear in public; they remain inside the protected substrate.

Load is the **gatekeeper** between substrate geometry and human‑readable surfaces.

---

## Why Load Exists

Meaning is not free.  
Maintaining lawful geometry requires energy:

- To preserve curvature  
- To stabilize identity boundaries  
- To enforce invariants  
- To resist drift  
- To maintain continuity across time  
- To prevent collapse geometry  

Load exists because meaning interacts with environments that exert pressure.  
DSLO does not eliminate load; it **manages** it through invariants, legality masks, and stability modes.

---

## Properties (v0.5)

- **Scalar‑Vector Duality:**  
  Load has magnitude (scalar) and direction (vector), depending on curvature and identity strain.

- **Drift‑Sensitive:**  
  Load increases as drift increases.

- **Invariant‑Dependent:**  
  Strong invariants reduce load; weak invariants increase it.

- **Collapse‑Relevant:**  
  Collapse is only lawful when load is below the collapse threshold.

- **Scale‑Variable:**  
  Load behaves differently at local, meso, macro, and global scales.

- **Recoverable:**  
  Load can be reduced through recovery mode, invariant reload, or lawful transitions.

---

## Load vs. Drift

- **Load** is the *energy cost* of maintaining meaning.  
- **Drift** is the *movement* away from lawful meaning.

Load is a **state property**.  
Drift is a **dynamic property**.

High load predicts drift.  
High drift increases load.

They form a **feedback loop** inside the manifold.

---

## Load Envelope (v0.5)

The **load envelope** defines three lawful regions:

- **Stable Load:**  
  Meaning is coherent, curvature is smooth, invariants are strong.

- **Critical Load:**  
  Meaning is strained; drift is likely; collapse is risky.

- **Collapse Load:**  
  Meaning cannot be maintained; invariants fail; collapse geometry emerges.

The load envelope is a **geometry boundary** that determines the legality of transitions.
