# Drift

## v0.5 Definition

**Drift** is the unlawful or unstable deviation of a semantic manifold region away from its invariant geometry.  
It is the movement of meaning, identity, or curvature under external or ecological forces that push a signal out of its lawful region, increasing semantic load and reducing stability.

Drift is not “error” in the conventional sense.  
It is a **geometry event**:

- Curvature bends beyond lawful bounds  
- Meaning load increases  
- Identity regions deform  
- Invariants weaken  
- Continuity becomes strained  

Drift is the opposite of collapse:  
collapse is a **controlled reduction**,  
drift is an **uncontrolled displacement**.

---

## Semantic Geometry Of Drift

**Semantic Manifold:**  
Drift occurs inside the manifold where meaning vectors, identity regions, and legality masks interact.  
A stable region has low curvature, low load, and strong invariants.  
Drift increases curvature and load, pushing the region toward instability.

**Curvature Pressure:**  
Drift manifests as curvature pressure—forces that distort the geometry of meaning.  
High curvature indicates instability; low curvature indicates lawful continuity.

**Load Accumulation:**  
Meaning load rises as drift increases.  
Load accumulation is a measurable quantity: the energy required to maintain meaning coherence under strain.

**Invariant Degradation:**  
Drift weakens invariants.  
When invariants degrade, legality masks begin to fail, and the manifold enters a critical or collapse‑adjacent state.

## Why Drift Exists

Meaning is not static.  
It interacts with:

- Ecological forces  
- Human interpretation  
- System load  
- Contextual changes  
- Identity pressure  
- Role boundaries  

Drift exists because meaning is embedded in environments that exert pressure.  
DSLO does not eliminate drift; it **manages** it through invariants, legality masks, and recovery geometry.

## Properties (v0.5)

- **Directional:**  
  Drift has magnitude and direction, represented as a **drift vector**.

- **Load‑Increasing:**  
  Drift raises meaning load, making stability harder to maintain.

- **Curvature‑Producing:**  
  Drift increases semantic curvature, pushing regions toward instability.

- **Invariant‑Weakening:**  
  Drift erodes invariants, reducing legality and continuity.

- **Recoverable:**  
  Drift can be corrected through recovery mode, fallback geometry, or invariant reload.

- **Scale‑Dependent:**  
  Drift behaves differently at local, meso, macro, and global scales.

---

## Drift vs. Collapse

- **Drift** is *movement away* from lawful geometry.  
- **Collapse** is *reduction into* a stable surface.

Drift is a **dynamic instability**.  
Collapse is a **static stabilization**.

Drift precedes collapse geometry when invariants fail.
